In this post, we derive the mean value of the negative binomial probability mass function for random variable \(Y\).

If \(Y\) is a random variable with a negative binomial probability distribution, then its probability mass function is given by the expression,

\[\begin{equation} P(Y=y) = p(y) = {y-1 \choose r-1 } p^r(1-p)^{y-r}. \tag{1} \end{equation}\]

We note that if we sum the pmf, Eq. (1), for values of \(y \ge r\), then the result totals to the value 1, as expected for a pmf.

\[\begin{equation} \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} {y-1 \choose r-1 } p^r(1-p)^{y-r} = 1. \tag{2} \end{equation}\]

The expected value \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) is defined as follows,

\[\begin{align} \mathbb{E}[Y] = & \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} y \cdot p(y) \\\\ = & \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} y \cdot {y-1 \choose r-1 } p^r(1-p)^{y-r}. \tag{3} \end{align}\]

From Eq. (3), our goal is to re-shape the summation so that it takes a form equivalent to Eq. (2).

We begin with our definition (repeated here) of the expected value from Eq. (3),

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} y\cdot {y-1 \choose r-1 } p^r(1-p)^{y-r}. \tag{4} \end{equation}\]

First, we expand our combination,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} y \cdot {\color{red} \frac{(y-1)!}{(r-1)! (y-r)!} } p^r(1-p)^{y-r}. \end{equation}\]

From here, the leading \(y\) combines with the factorial \((y-1)!\) to form the factorial \(y!\) in the numerator,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum^{\infty}_{ y=r } \frac{\color{red} y!}{ (r-1)! (y-r)!} p^r(1-p)^{y-r}. \end{equation}\]

Next, we pull out the parameter \(r\) from the summation,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = {\color{red} r } \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} \frac{y!}{{\color{red} r!} (y-r)!} p^r (1-p)^{y-r}. \end{equation}\]

Next again, we pull out the parameter \(1/p\) from the summation,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = \frac{r}{\color{red} p} \sum^{\infty}_{y=r} \frac{y!}{r! (y-r)!} p^{\color{red}r+1} (1-p)^{y-r}. \end{equation}\]

Now, let \(m-1=r\) in the summation,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = \frac rp \sum^{\infty}_{y={\color{red}m-1}} \frac{y!}{ {\color{red}(m-1)!} (y-{\color{red} m+1})!} p^m (1-p)^{y-{\color{red} m+1}}. \end{equation}\]

And now, let \(z-1 = y\) in the summation,

\[\begin{align} \mathbb{E}[Y] = & \frac rp \sum^{\infty}_{\color{red}z=m} \frac{\color{red}(z-1)!}{(m-1)! {\color{red}(z-m)!}} p^m (1-p)^{\color{red}z-m} \\\\ = & \frac rp \sum^{\infty}_{z=m} {z-1 \choose m-1} p^m (1-p)^{z-m} \tag{5} \\\\ = & \frac rp \cdot 1 \\\\ = & \frac rp. \end{align}\]

When you compare the summation in Eq. (5) to the summation in Eq. (2), you see that they take the same form if you equate \(z = y\) and \(m = r\). This means that the summation in Eq. (5) reduces to 1,

In this manner, we are left with our final mean value,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbb{E}[Y] = \frac rp. \end{equation}\]
