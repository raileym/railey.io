Why I want to be a data scientist

Last updated on Jan 1, 2021 4 min read
A Male Belted Kingfisher At Merritt Island by C Railey is licensed under CC BY 4.0

For some time, I have been writing software to help kids learn math. After so many years to that end, I decided I needed a change of venue: I like software engineering challenges but need a different path.

Specifically, I need to work on more complicated math and computer science; I want to use new programming languages, tools, and frameworks; and I want to work with various domains.

I believe working in data science will give me a new sense of direction. Plus, doing so will satisfy my work requirements more broadly:

  • Work remotely
  • Work in software engineering
  • Work in new areas in computer science
  • Work in a domain with boundless opportunity
  • Work with challenging math

Work remotely. Honestly, I don’t want to move. Of course, I can work locally, but then that would be limiting. Instead, I want to work remotely for whichever company I choose and from wherever I live. Remote work is no longer a limiting choice.

Work in software engineering. I like software engineering, from writing code to designing new architectures. I need more challenging problems, from working with complexity and uncertainty to solving scale and design problems. And I need more math.

I freely admit that writing production code (software engineering) is both demanding and rewarding. However, writing production code is not where I want to focus my energy; instead, I want to work with something new.

Work in new areas in computer science. I am ready to give artificial intelligence a try. I want to solve problems across the board: robotics, vision, natural language processing, planning, machine learning, and deep learning. I can combine my effort in artificial intelligence with my experience in software engineering.

I also want to work with data – lots of data. I am speaking about designing structures, writing algorithms, analyzing complexity, and working with architectures for scale and performance.

Work in a domain with boundless opportunity. When I was a consultant, I always told myself, “there is always room for one in the movie theater.” I don’t need to find the funding for 20 engineers; I only need to find the funding for one (me).

Then again, securing even one seat as a software engineer is hard: you need to write a lot of code. On the other hand, securing a seat as a data scientist could be easy (no production code required): you need a path to a strong recommendation.

Finding the path, not writing the code, is the difference. And, as for finding that path, the sky is the limit.

Work with challenging math. I like the idea of adding complicated math – applied statistics – to my work results. To that end, I am motivated to learn everything I can about applied statistics, machine learning, deep learning and then use today’s technology to build upon that knowledge. I find it interesting that old topics in statistics are now considered fresh and innovative thanks to technological advances.

Separately, I like knowing that the path I want to follow is a data scientist’s path leading to the most desired job. In their article for Harvard Business Review (Davenport and Patil 2012), Davenport and Patil describe the data scientist’s position as just that: “Data Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century.”

In their article, Davenport and Patil describe a point of innovation at LinkedIn when a scientist – Jonathon Goldman – recognized the implied social connections between LinkedIn’s users. Specifically, Goldman recognized the pattern of “triangle closing”, which is to say that “if you know Larry and Sue, there’s a good chance that Larry and Sue know each other” (Davenport and Patil 2012). And from that discovery, Goldman and his team gave birth to LinkedIn’s feature, “People You May Know,” which launched LinkedIn onto its meteoric path.

Goldman’s discovery stems from the data, not from the code – my point exactly.

References

Davenport, Thomas H., and D. J. Patil. 2012. “Data Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century.” https://hbr.org/2012/10/data-scientist-the-sexiest-job-of-the-21st-century.
